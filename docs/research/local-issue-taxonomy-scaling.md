# Auto-generating local political fault lines for 19,000 cities

**No one has built this yet — but the pieces exist.** The infrastructure to auto-generate hyper-local political issue taxonomies at scale is scattered across academic datasets, civic tech platforms, and NLP research, but no project has combined them into a working system covering thousands of US cities. The most promising technical approach is a hybrid pipeline: named entity recognition to surface local proper nouns, embedding-based clustering to find natural topic groupings, and LLM-based synthesis to produce interpretable "political fault line" labels — all anchored by the Legistar API, which provides structured agenda data for roughly 70% of America's largest cities. One year of meeting data (240–1,440 agenda items) is sufficient for most methods, and LLMs can generate usable taxonomies from as few as 50 agenda titles, solving the cold-start problem for data-sparse cities.

---

## The NLP pipeline that actually works for this

Traditional topic modeling alone won't produce the hyper-local fault lines you need. LDA generates bag-of-words topics like {housing, unit, affordable, development, plan} — useful but generic. BERTopic improves semantic coherence through transformer embeddings, and a 2024 PLOS ONE study validated it on government text, but it still trends toward policy categories rather than the entity-rich labels ("Point Molate," "Chevron & the Refinery") that define a city's politics.

The most directly relevant academic work is **Brady & Islam's "Iterative Topic Taxonomy Induction" (2025)**, which combines embedding-based clustering with constrained LLM decoding to generate political issue taxonomies without predefined labels — tested on Meta political ads before the 2024 election. Microsoft's **TnT-LLM framework (KDD 2024)** offers a scalable complement: use an LLM to generate and iteratively refine a taxonomy, then train lightweight classifiers (logistic regression on embeddings) for large-scale labeling at a fraction of per-document LLM cost. **TopicGPT (NAACL 2024)** achieved a harmonic mean purity of **0.74 on Congressional bills** versus 0.64 for BERTopic/LDA, though it requires GPT-4-class models for the taxonomy generation step.

The recommended architecture is a **four-stage hybrid pipeline**. First, run NER (spaCy or fine-tuned transformers) to extract place names, organizations, facilities, and legal references — the proper nouns that anchor local fault lines. Second, embed agenda items using sentence-transformers and cluster via UMAP + HDBSCAN to find natural topic groupings. Third, feed cluster representatives (top keywords, sample documents, extracted entities) to an LLM using iterative constrained decoding — the LLM either assigns an existing label or proposes a new one, contextualizing raw entities into fault-line labels like "Chevron & the Refinery." Fourth, train a lightweight classifier (SetFit or fine-tuned sentence transformer) for ongoing classification. Providing Richmond's hand-curated taxonomy as few-shot examples guides the style of output.

A critical finding from Campos et al.'s 2026 survey paper, "NLP for Local Governance Meeting Records," is that **the main obstacles are structural, not algorithmic** — heterogeneity across municipalities in how they format and publish records is the binding constraint, not the NLP methods themselves.

## Data sources ranked by scalability and signal

Meeting minutes and agendas are the primary source, but several complementary datasets can sharpen taxonomy generation, especially for cities with sparse records.

**Tier 1 sources** combine broad national coverage with programmatic access and strong signal for political fault lines. The **Legistar Web API** (`webapi.legistar.com`) exposes structured agenda data — matters, events, votes, committee assignments — for roughly 70% of the largest US cities and counties. The open-source `python-legistar-scraper` is the standard tool, and both Councilmatic and Council Data Project rely on it. **GDELT's DOC 2.0 API** offers free full-text search across a rolling three-month window of global news coverage with geographic tagging and sentiment analysis — useful for detecting when a local issue spikes in salience. **Ballotpedia's API** provides structured ballot measure data (topic, text, arguments for/against, vote results) that represent the purest expression of local political fault lines — literally the issues voters decide on. The **EPA's Envirofacts and ECHO databases** identify regulated facilities, violations, and Superfund sites by location at national scale, surfacing the environmental controversies (like Richmond's Chevron refinery) that drive local politics.

**Tier 2 sources** offer strong signal where available but cover fewer cities. The **Newscatcher Local News API** uses six location-detection methods to surface hyperlocal news from thousands of small publishers. **SeeClickFix/Open311** complaint data (available for several hundred cities) reveals quality-of-life issues through geographic clustering of resident reports. **Zoneomics and Regrid** provide national parcel and zoning data via API, giving spatial context for development controversies.

**Tier 3 sources** carry the highest signal but the lowest scalability. Patch.com covers roughly 1,900 communities with genuinely hyperlocal political reporting but has no API. Local campaign finance data for municipal races is the most fragmented category in American civic data — **no national database exists**, and filing requirements vary by state, county, and city. Written public comments, increasingly submitted via Legistar's eComment system, are extraordinarily rich but published inconsistently. League of Women Voters voter guides provide hand-curated issue lists for specific jurisdictions but aren't machine-readable.

## How to validate that you found the right 14 issues

Validation is the hardest unsolved problem here. **No ground-truth dataset of city-specific political fault lines exists for US cities**, and standard automated coherence metrics are unreliable for this domain. Hoyle et al. (NeurIPS 2021) demonstrated that coherence scores like C_v and NPMI don't reliably correlate with human judgments, especially for domain-specific topics — and hyper-local proper nouns won't have meaningful co-occurrence statistics in general reference corpora.

The closest methodological precedent is the **Aarhus Research Group's work on Danish municipal agendas** — they applied the Comparative Agendas Project framework to **250,000 agenda items across 98 Danish municipalities** using a 189-category codebook, tracking topic attention over time. No equivalent US local-level project exists, which represents a significant gap. The **keyATM model** (Eshima, Imai & Sasaki, 2024, *American Journal of Political Science*) offers a promising semi-supervised approach: researchers specify seed keywords per topic, and the model discovers the rest, validated against human-coded Congressional bills using AUROC. This bridges unsupervised discovery with domain-guided validation and is directly applicable — seed keywords could come from hand-curated taxonomies like Richmond's.

A practical multi-layer validation strategy would combine four approaches:

- **Computational screening**: Coverage metrics (what percentage of substantive agenda items get classified — target >65%), temporal stability testing (do the same topics emerge from different two-year windows), and split-half reliability
- **Cross-reference validation**: Compare auto-generated taxonomies against Ballotpedia ballot measure topics, LocalView transcript distributions, NLC State of the Cities priority categories, and mayoral State of the City addresses
- **Human expert review**: For a stratified sample of 50–100 cities, recruit local journalists, council staff, or community organizers to evaluate taxonomies using word intrusion and topic intrusion tasks, targeting Krippendorff's α > 0.7
- **Benchmark construction**: Building validated gold-standard taxonomies for even 100 diverse cities would be a **novel contribution to the field** — publishable in venues like *Political Analysis* or *Scientific Data*

For the precision-recall tradeoff, **missing an important fault line (low recall) is worse** than including a marginal one (low precision), since the platform's core value proposition is comprehensive local political mapping. Optimize for recall first, then prune via human review.

## The dataset landscape is sparse but growing

The **LocalView dataset** (Barari & Simko, 2023, *Nature Scientific Data*) is the single most relevant resource: **139,616 YouTube meeting videos with transcripts from 1,012 places and 2,861 governments across 49 states (2006–2022)**, hosted on Harvard Dataverse. MIT's Center for Constructive Communication is actively applying LLMs to LocalView for summarization, topic modeling, and search. **MeetingBank** (ACL 2023) provides a complementary benchmark: 1,366 city council meetings from six major US cities with aligned transcripts and professional minutes, averaging 28,000 tokens per meeting.

Among research organizations, the **National League of Cities** publishes annual State of the Cities reports synthesizing mayoral priorities, but these are aggregate national summaries of generic categories (infrastructure, housing, public safety), not city-specific fault lines. **ICMA** surveys roughly 11,000 municipalities on governance practices and service delivery but not political controversies. The **Urban Institute, Brookings, Pew, and Lincoln Institute** produce valuable policy research but maintain no structured database mapping political issues to specific cities. **Ballotpedia** covers elections comprehensively for the top 100 US cities and all state capitals, including local ballot measures with topic classifications — the closest thing to a structured fault-line database, but limited in geographic scope.

The **Council Data Project** (University of Washington) provides open-source infrastructure for curating municipal meeting data, deployed in Seattle, Portland, Oakland, and roughly a dozen other cities, with a goal of reaching the 50 largest US cities. **Curate (now FiscalNote)** is the most commercially mature player, scanning meeting minutes and agendas from over **12,000 local government entities** — but their data is proprietary and geared toward enterprise compliance monitoring. **CivicBand** covers roughly 1,010 municipalities with 12.6 million pages of meeting data. City Bureau's **Documenters** network trains and pays community members to attend and document meetings across 22+ communities, producing Creative Commons meeting notes that could serve as NLP training data.

The central finding across all these sources: **no comprehensive database of local political fault lines exists for US cities**. Building one at scale would be genuinely novel.

## One year of data is enough — and LLMs solve the cold start

The minimum data requirements vary sharply by method. For LDA with 14 topics, Tang et al. (2014, ICML) establish a turning point around **1,000–2,000 documents**, with diminishing returns after 5,000. BERTopic's implicit minimum — driven by HDBSCAN's default `min_cluster_size` of 10 — is roughly **200–300 documents** for 14 topics. Since a typical city council produces 240–1,440 agenda items per year, **one year of data is sufficient for BERTopic** in most cities, while LDA requires two to three years.

Agenda item titles (5–15 words) present the classic short-text topic modeling challenge. Specialized models outperform LDA here: **GSDMM** (Gibbs Sampling Dirichlet Multinomial Mixture) assumes one topic per document and automatically infers cluster count, while **BTM** (Biterm Topic Model) operates on corpus-level word co-occurrence patterns rather than document-level statistics. BERTopic handles short text better than LDA because sentence-transformer embeddings capture semantic meaning even in brief phrases, though HDBSCAN may produce excessive outliers — switching to k-Means clustering forces all documents into topics and avoids this. A practical enrichment strategy is **meeting-level document pooling**: concatenating all agenda items from one meeting into a single document, analogous to the account-level aggregation shown to dramatically improve topic coherence on tweets.

The cold-start problem is where LLMs transform the landscape. **TopicGen** (Wanrooij et al., 2024) explicitly targets corpora under 1,000 documents and outperformed BERTopic, LDA, and NMF on 800 NYT articles using GPT-4o. For cities with only 50–100 agenda items, direct LLM prompting — feeding all titles in a single context window and asking for recurring political fault lines — produces usable taxonomies in seconds. An ACL 2024 study confirmed LLM-generated topics achieve **>95% factuality** (rarely hallucinating topics not present in the data) with higher coherence scores than BERTopic on short text.

The practical architecture for 19,000 cities is a **tiered system**. Roughly 2,000 cities with over 1,000 agenda items get the full BERTopic hybrid pipeline with city-specific taxonomies. Another 5,000 cities with 200–1,000 items use BERTopic with k-Means clustering plus LLM refinement. Approximately 5,000 cities with 20–200 items use LLM-based TopicGen approaches. The remaining 7,000 cities with fewer than 20 items or no digital records receive **transfer taxonomies** bootstrapped from demographically and geographically similar cities — clustered by population, region, governance type (council-manager vs. mayor-council), and economic base. A universal seed taxonomy derived from the Comparative Agendas Project framework provides the starting point, progressively refined as local data accumulates.

OCR is no longer a bottleneck. **Mistral OCR 3** processes 2,000 pages per minute at $1–2 per 1,000 pages with >99% character accuracy on printed text. Processing five years of a city's meeting minutes costs under a dollar. The real bottleneck is finding and downloading the PDFs from thousands of heterogeneous municipal websites.

## The civic tech ecosystem has pieces but no integrated solution

**Councilmatic** (DataMade, 2011–present) remains the canonical open-source municipal legislation tracker, built on the Legistar API and the Open Civic Data standard, deployed in Chicago, NYC, and several other cities. But it performs no topic classification — it's a data presentation and search tool. **Open States** covers all 50 state legislatures but never expanded to local government. **Plural Policy** (formerly Civic Eagle, acquired by SAI360 in December 2025) offers AI-powered bill topic classification and summarization for state and federal legislation — the closest commercial analog to auto-generated issue taxonomies — but does not cover municipal government.

The **Granicus ecosystem** is the critical infrastructure layer. Legistar's agenda management software serves roughly 70% of the largest US cities, and its public Web API is the single most important programmatic access point to municipal legislative data. Granicus's acquisition of PrimeGov (now OneMeeting) is consolidating the market further. However, Granicus is a vendor, not a transparency platform — their data serves municipal clerks, not civic engagement.

The most relevant academic group is the **University of Washington Information School**, which maintains the Council Data Project. The most relevant dataset creators are **Barari and Simko** (LocalView) at Harvard/Columbia. The most relevant recent survey paper is **Campos et al.'s "NLP for Local Governance Meeting Records" (2026)**, which comprehensively reviews three core NLP tasks for municipal records. International parallels include **mySociety's TheyWorkForYou** (UK Parliament monitoring), **OpenPolis** (Italian Parliament), and the **Japanese Regional Assembly Minutes Corpus** — but none address city-level political fault line extraction.

## Conclusion

The path to auto-generating political fault lines for 19,000 cities runs through a hybrid NLP pipeline (NER + BERTopic + LLM synthesis), anchored by Legistar API data and supplemented by GDELT news monitoring, Ballotpedia ballot measures, and EPA facility data. LLMs fundamentally change the economics: they reduce minimum data requirements from thousands of documents to dozens, produce interpretable entity-rich labels rather than word bags, and enable a tiered deployment where even data-sparse cities get usable taxonomies. The Brady & Islam iterative taxonomy induction framework and Microsoft's TnT-LLM provide the most directly applicable architectural templates. Validation remains the hardest challenge — no US ground truth exists at the local level, making the construction of even a 100-city benchmark dataset a publishable contribution. The Aarhus group's work on Danish municipal agendas and the keyATM semi-supervised model offer the strongest methodological foundations for building that benchmark. Perhaps the most important strategic insight: **Curate (FiscalNote) already covers 12,000+ local government entities** with meeting data, and LocalView provides transcripts from 1,012 places — meaning the raw text corpus for a significant fraction of the target 19,000 cities may already exist, awaiting the right NLP pipeline to transform it into structured political intelligence.
