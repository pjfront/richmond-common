# California Ethics & Conflict of Interest Laws for Local Officials

**Research Date:** 2026-03-16
**Purpose:** Inform Richmond Commons' conflict scanner with accurate legal thresholds, classifications, and enforcement context.
**Applies to:** City council members, board/commission members, and candidates in California cities (specifically Richmond, CA).

---

## 1. The Levine Act (Government Code Section 84308)

### 1.1 Background and Expansion

The Levine Act was enacted in 1982 after California Coastal Commission members were found soliciting large contributions from applicants with pending matters. Originally applied only to **appointed** officials. **SB 1439 (2022)** expanded it to all local elected officials (city council members, county supervisors, school board members) effective January 1, 2023. This was the most significant expansion in the Act's 40-year history.

**SB 1243 (Dodd) and SB 1181 (Glazer)** made further amendments effective **January 1, 2025**. FPPC adopted implementing regulations on **March 20, 2025**.

Sources:
- [FPPC Pay-to-Play Page](https://www.fppc.ca.gov/learn/pay-to-play-limits-and-prohibitions/)
- [Nossaman: 2025 Updates](https://www.nossaman.com/newsroom-insights-important-updates-to-californias-pay-to-play-contribution-bans-go-into-effect-in-2025)
- [Baker Manock: 2025 Revisions](https://bakermanock.com/2025-revisions-levine-act/)

### 1.2 Contribution Threshold

| Period | Threshold | Authority |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| 1982-2024 | $250 (aggregate per candidate) | Original Section 84308 |
| **2025 onward** | **$500 (aggregate per candidate)** | SB 1243, effective Jan 1, 2025 |

**Critical for our system:** The threshold is **$500** as of 2025. Contributions of $500 or below do **not** trigger the Act's recusal requirements.

### 1.3 What Triggers the Act: "Entitlement for Use" Proceedings

Section 84308 applies to proceedings involving a **"license, permit, or other entitlement for use."** The statute defines this as:

> All business, professional, trade and land use licenses and permits and all other entitlements for use, including all entitlements for land use, all contracts (other than competitively bid, labor, or personal employment contracts), and all franchises.

**What IS covered:**
- Land use permits (conditional use permits, variances, subdivisions)
- Non-competitively-bid contracts
- Business licenses and permits
- Franchises
- Any proceeding affecting specific, identifiable individuals with significant financial impact

**What is NOT covered:**
- General policy decisions or rulemaking (legislative acts)
- Proceedings where "interests affected are many and diverse"
- Competitively bid contracts "required by law, agency policy, or agency rule to be awarded pursuant to a competitive process" (clarified by 2025 amendments)
- Labor contracts
- Personal employment contracts

**Key question answered: Do commission/board appointments count?**
**No.** Per FPPC guidance, appointments to boards, commissions, and committees are a form of employment contract, which is **exempt** from the definition of "entitlement for use." Section 84308 does not prohibit a mayor from appointing someone who donated >$500.

Sources:
- [Palo Alto Levine Act Memo (Dec 2024)](https://www.paloalto.gov/files/content/public/v/8/departments/city-attorney/resources/levine-act-memo_-dec.-12-2024.pdf)
- [FPPC Section 84308 Staff Memo (May 2023)](https://www.fppc.ca.gov/content/dam/fppc/NS-Documents/AgendaDocuments/lawandpolicy/2023/may/84308%20Staff%20Memo%205.8.23.pdf)
- [BBK Law: FPPC Guidance on SB 1439](https://bbklaw.com/resources/la-072023-fppc-provides-new-guidance-regarding-sb-1439-levine-act-bill)

### 1.4 Party vs. Participant vs. Agent

| Role | Definition | Contribution Rules |
|------|------------|-------------------|
| **Party** | Person who files an application for, or is the subject of, an entitlement proceeding | Contributions >$500 trigger recusal |
| **Participant** | Person who is NOT a party but actively supports/opposes a decision AND has a financial interest in the decision. Must lobby in person, testify, or otherwise act to influence officers. | Same contribution rules as party |
| **Agent** | Person paid to represent a party/participant who has communicated with the agency to influence the proceeding | Contributions tracked but **no longer aggregated** with principal's contributions (2025 change) |

**Business entity/officer question:** When a business is a party, its officers and agents may independently trigger the Act if they qualify as "agents" (i.e., paid representatives who communicated with the agency). However, as of 2025, **agent contributions are no longer aggregated** with those of the party/participant — this is a significant change from prior law (SB 1243 amended subdivision (g)).

Contributions are aggregated under Gov. Code Section 82015.5 rules. Non-compensated nonprofit officers' contributions are NOT aggregated.

Source: [FPPC Parties, Participants, Agents Guide](https://www.fppc.ca.gov/content/dam/fppc/NS-Documents/TAD/Campaign%20Documents/84308/Section%2084308%20and%20Parties%20and%20Participants%20Guide.pdf)

### 1.5 Lookback Period

**12 months preceding the decision.** An officer must recuse if they received >$500 from a party, participant, or agent within the 12 months before the proceeding.

Additionally:
- No officer shall accept/solicit/direct contributions >$500 from a party or participant **while a proceeding is pending** and **for 12 months following the date of the final decision**.
- A proceeding is "pending" when: (1) the decision is before the officer, OR (2) it is reasonably foreseeable the decision will come before the officer and they know or have reason to know it's within the agency's jurisdiction.

### 1.6 Disclosure and Recusal Procedures

**Disclosure:** Before rendering any decision, each officer who received a triggering contribution must disclose that fact **on the record** — orally or in writing, in the public meeting, at the beginning of the meeting involving the proceeding.

**Parties/participants** must also disclose: Any contribution >$500 (aggregated) within the preceding 12 months, including contributions by their agent and those required to be aggregated under Section 82015.5. Disclosure must be on the record **at least 30 days before the decision** (per 2025 FPPC regulations).

**Recusal:** Officer who willfully or knowingly received a triggering contribution **shall not make, participate in making, or in any way attempt to use their official position to influence** the decision.

**Cure provision:** An officer may cure a violation and participate if:
1. They did NOT knowingly and willfully accept the contribution
2. They disclose the contribution amount and contributor name
3. They **return** the amount exceeding $500 within **30 days** (extended from 14 days by 2025 amendments) from when they knew or should have known about both the contribution and the proceeding

### 1.7 Appointing Body (City Council) vs. Decision-Making Body

**The Levine Act applies to the appointing body, not just the final decision-maker.** This is a critical finding:

Because the City Council exercises authority or budgetary control over bodies like the Planning Commission, contributions to Council members are subject to Section 84308 when those lower bodies have pending entitlement proceedings. An "officer" includes anyone who "exercises authority or budgetary control over the agency of officers who may" make the entitlement decision.

**Practical example:** A developer with a permit application before the Planning Commission cannot contribute >$500 to City Council members who exercise authority over the Commission.

Source: [City of Lincoln Levine Act FAQ](https://www.lincolnca.gov/media/3nelsr1a/final-faq-levine-act.pdf)

### 1.8 Exemptions (2025 Updates)

- **City Attorney/County Counsel** providing legal advice but without decision-making authority: exempt from "officer" definition
- **Competitively bid contracts** required by law, policy, or rule: exempt
- **Participants whose financial interest results solely from membership dues changes:** exempt

## 2. Political Reform Act Financial Conflicts (Gov. Code Sections 87100-87105)

### 2.1 The Basic Rule (Section 87100)

A public official **must disqualify** from any decision in which they have a **reasonably foreseeable material financial effect**, distinguishable from the effect on the public generally, on:
- The official directly
- The official's immediate family
- Any of their financial interests (see below)

This is **broader than the Levine Act** — it applies to ALL governmental decisions, not just entitlement proceedings.

### 2.2 Types of Financial Interests Triggering Recusal

| Interest Type | Threshold | Authority |
|---------------|-----------|-----------|
| **Real property** | $2,000+ interest value | Section 87103 |
| **Business entity (investment)** | $2,000+ investment | Section 87103 |
| **Business entity (position)** | Director, officer, partner, trustee, or employee | Section 87103 |
| **Source of income** | $500+ within 12 months | Section 87103 |
| **Gifts** | $630+ from single source in calendar year (2025-2026) | Adjusted biennially by CPI |
| **Personal finances** | Direct effect on personal finances | Section 87103 |

### 2.3 Materiality Standards (FPPC Regulation 18702)

The FPPC has adopted detailed materiality standards that determine when a foreseeable financial effect is significant enough to trigger recusal.

**If the interest is "explicitly involved"** (the interest is a named party or subject of the decision): the financial effect is **presumed to be both foreseeable and material**.

**If the interest is "not explicitly involved"**: materiality depends on the type of interest, with specific standards for each:

#### The "500-Foot Rule" (Regulation 18702.2) — Real Property

| Distance | Standard |
|----------|----------|
| **Within 500 feet** | **Presumption** that the decision will have a material effect. Rebutted only by "clear and convincing evidence" it won't have a substantial impact. |
| **500-1,000 feet** | Material if the decision would change: market value, development potential, income-producing potential, highest/best use, or character (traffic, intensity, parking, view, privacy, noise, air quality) |
| **Beyond 1,000 feet** | Generally not material (case-by-case analysis) |

The 500-foot rule was revised in 2019 and **is still current** as of 2025.

#### Income/Investment Thresholds

- Source of income (individual): material if decision affects individual's income, investments, or assets/liabilities by **$1,000+**
- Business entity income: material if decision affects entity's income by specific thresholds in Regulation 18702.3
- Real property income: $500+ rental income in 12 months presumed

Sources:
- [FPPC Regulation 18702.2 (Real Property)](https://www.fppc.ca.gov/content/dam/fppc/NS-Documents/LegalDiv/Regulations/Index/Chapter7/Article2/18702.2%20Materiality%20Standard-%20Financial%20Interest%20in%20Real%20Property%206.27.19.pdf)
- [Shute Mihaly: 500-Foot Rule Tips](https://www.smwlaw.com/2019/08/29/tips-for-complying-with-the-fppcs-revised-500-foot-rule-addressing-when-public-officials-have-a-conflict-of-interest-in-decisions-potentially-affecting-their-real-property/)
- [RWG Law: New 500-Foot Rule](https://www.rwglaw.com/newsroom-news-500-foot-rule-conflict-of-interest-real-property)

### 2.4 How Sections 87100-87105 Differ from the Levine Act

| Feature | Levine Act (Section 84308) | PRA Financial Conflicts (Section 87100) |
|---------|---------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Trigger** | Campaign contributions >$500 | Any financial interest with material effect |
| **Scope** | Only "entitlement for use" proceedings | ALL governmental decisions |
| **Who's affected** | Parties, participants, agents in proceedings | The official's own financial interests |
| **Threshold** | $500 contribution threshold | Various materiality standards (see above) |
| **Lookback** | 12 months | Varies by interest type |
| **Remedy** | Can cure by returning contribution within 30 days | No cure — must recuse |

### 2.5 Recusal Procedure (Section 87105)

For officials specified in Section 87200 (elected officials, certain appointed officials), upon identifying a conflict they must **immediately prior to consideration**:
1. **Publicly identify** the financial interest in detail sufficient to be understood by the public
2. **Recuse** from discussing and voting
3. **Leave the room** until after discussion, vote, and disposition — unless the matter is on the uncontested portion of the agenda

Source: [Cal. Gov. Code Section 87105](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=87105.&lawCode=GOV)

## 3. Quasi-Judicial vs. Legislative Proceedings

### 3.1 The Core Distinction

| Feature | Legislative | Quasi-Judicial |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| **Purpose** | Create new policy/rules for future application | Apply existing rules to specific facts |
| **Scope** | Broad, community-wide | Narrow, specific parties affected |
| **Examples** | Zoning ordinances, budgets, general plans | Variances, CUPs, site-specific permits |
| **Ex parte contacts** | Generally permitted | **Prohibited** |
| **Standard of review** | Political discretion | Substantial evidence |
| **Bias rules** | Less restrictive | Strict impartiality required |
| **Hearing purpose** | Gather public opinion | Gather evidence |
| **Due process** | Minimal ("ballot box") | Full (notice, opportunity to be heard) |

### 3.2 Specific Action Classifications

| Action | Classification | Notes |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| **Adoption of zoning ordinance** | Legislative | Applies broadly to future cases |
| **General plan amendment** | Legislative | Policy-level decision |
| **Budget appropriations** | Legislative | General policy, many and diverse interests |
| **Site-specific rezoning** | **Quasi-judicial** | Applies existing criteria to specific property/applicant |
| **Conditional use permits** | Quasi-judicial | Specific applicant, specific property |
| **Variances** | Quasi-judicial | Specific applicant, specific property |
| **Subdivision approvals** | Quasi-judicial | Specific applicant |
| **License revocations** | Quasi-judicial | Specific party |
| **Nuisance abatement** | Quasi-judicial | Specific property/party |
| **Commission appointments** | **Neither** (employment) | Exempt from Levine Act entirely |
| **Area-wide rezoning** | Legislative | Broad, many interests affected |

### 3.3 The Rezoning Question

Rezoning can be **either** legislative or quasi-judicial depending on scope:
- **Area-wide/general rezoning** (e.g., updating zoning for an entire district) = **legislative**
- **Site-specific rezoning** (e.g., rezoning a particular parcel at an owner's request) = **quasi-judicial**

The test: Does the action apply pre-existing criteria to a specific fact situation (quasi-judicial), or does it establish new policy for future application (legislative)?

### 3.4 Why This Matters for the Levine Act

The Levine Act applies only to **entitlement for use** proceedings, which are generally quasi-judicial in nature. Legislative proceedings (general policy, rulemaking) where interests affected are "many and diverse" are **excluded** from Section 84308.

However, the **PRA financial conflicts (Section 87100)** apply to ALL governmental decisions — legislative AND quasi-judicial.

Sources:
- [League of California Cities: Common Issues in Quasi-Judicial Proceedings](https://www.cacities.org/Resources-Documents/Member-Engagement/Professional-Departments/City-Attorneys/Library/2013/2013-Annual-Conference-City-Attorneys-Track/9-2013-Annunal-Adam-U-Lindgren-Common-Issues-in-Qu)

## 4. AB 571 and Campaign Contribution Limits

### 4.1 State Default Limits (AB 571)

AB 571 (Mullin, 2019), effective January 1, 2021, established **default** campaign contribution limits for local candidates where the city or county has NOT enacted its own limits:

| Period | Default Limit per Election | Authority |
|--------|---------------------------|-----------|
| 2023-2024 | $5,500 | FPPC CPI adjustment |
| **2025-2026** | **$5,900 per election** | FPPC CPI adjustment |

"Per election" means primary and general are separate — so effectively **$11,800 per candidate per cycle** for an individual donor.

AB 571 does **not** apply if a city or county has enacted its own contribution limits.

Source: [FPPC: Contribution Limits - City and County Candidates](https://www.fppc.ca.gov/learn/Contribution-Limits-City-and-County-Candidates.html)

### 4.2 Contra Costa County

Contra Costa County has its own ordinance with a **$2,500 per election cycle** limit for county-level offices (supervisor, assessor, DA, etc.). This supersedes the state default.

Source: [FPPC: Contra Costa County Campaign Ordinance](https://fppc.ca.gov/content/dam/fppc/NS-Documents/TAD/Campaign%20Ordinances/Counties/R_Contra_Costa.pdf)

### 4.3 City of Richmond (Verified from Municode, 2026-03-16)

Richmond has its own local campaign finance ordinances (Chapter 2.42 "Fair Elections" and Chapter 2.43 "Public Campaign Financing"). These **supersede** state AB 571 defaults.

#### Contribution Limits (Section 2.42.050)

- **$2,500 per person per election cycle** to any candidate or independent expenditure committee
- Self-funding exemption: candidate's own funds are NOT subject to the $2,500 limit
- Anonymous contributions of $100+ are prohibited
- Written records required for all contributions of $100+

**Note:** This is significantly lower than the state AB 571 default of $5,900/election. Richmond's $2,500 limit controls.

#### Pay-to-Play Restrictions

**Chapter 2.42 does NOT contain local pay-to-play restrictions.** There are no provisions restricting contributions from contractors, permit applicants, or parties with pending city proceedings. Any such restrictions come from state law (Levine Act § 84308). The earlier research referencing a 2012 ordinance mirroring the Levine Act at $250 may have been in a separate ordinance or has since been removed.

#### Matching Public Funds Program (Chapter 2.43)

- **Eligibility:** Candidate must accept no more than **$75,000 total contributions** for the election. Exceeding $75,000 disqualifies and requires returning all matching funds.
- **Match formula (Sec. 2.43.030):** Disbursed in $2,500 increments at contribution thresholds ($10K, $15K, $20K, $25K, $30K)
- **Maximum per candidate: $12,500 in matching funds per election**
- **Contingent on City Council budget appropriation** — not guaranteed
- Not matchable: loans, in-kind contributions, contributions received before July 1 of election year
- Note: Prior research cited "$25,000 matching / $40,000 cap" — this appears outdated. Current figures (as amended through Ord. 16-20, July 2020) are $12,500 max matching / $75,000 contribution cap.

#### Other Notable Provisions

- **Designated committee requirement (Sec. 2.42.070(b)):** Each candidate must name exactly ONE designated committee. Cannot be changed.
- **50% ownership disclosure (Sec. 2.42.070(c)):** Must disclose persons holding 50%+ ownership in non-individual contributors of $100+
- **Mass mailing disclosure (Sec. 2.42.075):** Committees spending $5,000+ in independent expenditures must disclose top 3 contributors on front page
- **Penalties (Sec. 2.42.080):** Up to **$5,000 per violation or 3x the unlawful amount** (whichever is greater). Candidates and treasurers personally liable. Civil enforcement by City Attorney or residents (60-day notice required).

- [Richmond Municipal Code Chapter 2.43](https://library.municode.com/ca/richmond/codes/code_of_ordinances?nodeId=ARTIIADGO_CH2.43PUCAFI)

## 5. Enforcement and Remedies

### 5.1 Levine Act Violations — Consequences

**Administrative penalties:**
- The FPPC closes 800-1,200 cases annually; hundreds result in administrative penalties and fines
- Warning letters for low-seriousness violations with minimal public harm
- Full investigations when sufficient evidence of violation exists

**Civil remedies (Gov. Code Section 91003(b)):**
- A resident can bring an action to restrain execution of official action taken in violation
- Court **may** grant preliminary restraining order pending adjudication
- If a violation is found **AND** "the official action might not otherwise have been taken or approved," the court **may set the action aside as void**
- Court must weigh injury to innocent persons relying on the official action

**Key legal point:** Decisions are **voidable, not automatically void.** Judicial action is required. The court has discretion and must find both (a) a violation occurred and (b) the outcome might have been different.

**Criminal penalties:** Willful violations can result in criminal prosecution.

### 5.2 Government Code Section 1090 Violations — Contracts

**Far more severe than Levine Act violations:**
- Contracts made in violation are **void ab initio** (automatically void, not just voidable)
- Willful violation is a **felony** punishable by state prison
- **Lifetime bar from holding public office** in California
- The "conflict for one is a conflict for all" rule: if ONE board member has a Section 1090 conflict, the **entire board** is prohibited from acting on the contract (unless a statutory exception applies)
- No cure by recusal alone (unlike PRA conflicts) — the board simply cannot act

### 5.3 PRA Financial Conflict Violations (Section 87100)

- Administrative fines by FPPC
- Civil penalties
- Criminal penalties for knowing/willful violations
- Decisions may be set aside under Section 91003(b) (same framework as Levine Act)

### 5.4 FPPC Advisory Letters on Commission Appointments

The FPPC has issued 12+ formal advice letters on Section 84308 since SB 1439 passed. Key finding relevant to commission appointments:

**Appointments to boards, commissions, and committees are exempt from the Levine Act** because they are considered a form of employment contract, which is excluded from the definition of "entitlement for use."

However, once appointed, commission members **are** "officers" subject to the Levine Act when their commission handles entitlement proceedings.

Source: [BBK Law: FPPC Guidance on SB 1439](https://bbklaw.com/resources/la-072023-fppc-provides-new-guidance-regarding-sb-1439-levine-act-bill)

## 6. Government Code Section 1090 — Financial Interest in Contracts

### 6.1 Overview

Section 1090 is **separate from and stricter than** the Levine Act. It prohibits any public officer or employee from having a financial interest in contracts they participate in making.

### 6.2 Key Differences from Levine Act

| Feature | Levine Act (Section 84308) | Section 1090 |
|---------|---------------------------|-------------|
| **Trigger** | Campaign contributions | Any financial interest in a contract |
| **Scope** | Entitlement proceedings | Contracts only |
| **Remedy** | Voidable (court discretion) | **Void ab initio** (automatic) |
| **Cure** | Return contribution within 30 days | **No cure** |
| **Recusal** | Recusal resolves the conflict | Recusal does NOT resolve — entire board is prohibited |
| **Criminal penalty** | Varies | **Felony + lifetime office ban** |

### 6.3 Interaction with Campaign Contributions

Section 1090 does **not** directly address campaign contributions — it addresses financial interests in contracts. However:
- If a contribution creates an expectation of benefit that rises to a financial interest, it could theoretically trigger Section 1090
- In practice, campaign contributions are primarily regulated by the Levine Act (Section 84308) and the PRA (Section 87100), not Section 1090
- The key risk is when a contributor receives a contract — the question is whether the official has a financial interest in that contract, not whether they received a contribution

### 6.4 Remote Interest Exception

If the official's interest is "remote" (as defined in Section 1091), the contract may proceed if:
1. The officer **discloses** the interest to the public agency
2. The interest is **noted in official records**
3. The officer **abstains** from participation

### 6.5 Recent Legislation

- **SB 1111:** Recognizes financial interests of adult children as "remote interests" for Section 1090 purposes
- **AB 3130:** Extends disclosure requirements to broader family relationships for county supervisors

Sources:
- [FPPC: Section 1090 Overview (Jan 2024)](https://www.fppc.ca.gov/content/dam/fppc/NS-Documents/LegalDiv/section-1090/Section%201090%20-%20Overview%20-%20Jan%202024.pdf)

## 7. Other Relevant Statutes

### 7.1 Government Code Section 87200 — Statutory Filers

Designates elected officials and certain appointed officials as "statutory filers" who must file Form 700 (Statement of Economic Interests) based on all disclosure categories — not limited by a conflict of interest code. All Richmond City Council members are Section 87200 filers.

### 7.2 Government Code Section 87300 — Conflict of Interest Codes

Requires each agency to adopt a conflict of interest code designating positions that involve decision-making with potential material financial effects, and specifying what types of interests those positions must disclose. This covers staff and commissioners not covered by Section 87200.

### 7.3 Government Code Section 87105 — Recusal Procedure

The specific procedural requirements for recusal: public identification of the interest, recusal from discussion/vote, and leaving the room (detailed in Section 2.5 above).

## 8. Summary: Thresholds for Our System

### Thresholds to Encode

| Law | Threshold | What It Triggers | Lookback |
|-----|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| **Levine Act (Section 84308)** | >$500 aggregate per candidate | Disclosure + recusal from entitlement proceedings | 12 months |
| **PRA Financial Conflict (Section 87100)** | Various materiality standards | Recusal from ANY governmental decision | Depends on interest type |
| **Real property (Regulation 18702.2)** | $2,000+ interest AND within 500 ft | Presumed material conflict | Ongoing |
| **Source of income** | $500+ in 12 months | Potential conflict | 12 months |
| **Investment** | $2,000+ | Potential conflict | Ongoing |
| **Gifts** | $630+ per source per year (2025-2026) | Potential conflict | Calendar year |
| **Section 1090** | Any financial interest | Contract is void; no cure | Ongoing |
| **AB 571 default contribution limit** | $5,900 per election (2025-2026) | Donation limit (not conflict trigger) | Per election |

### Classification Logic for the Scanner

1. **Is this an entitlement proceeding?** (permit, license, non-competitive contract, franchise, land use) -> Levine Act applies
2. **Is this a legislative action?** (ordinance, general plan, budget, area-wide rezoning) -> Levine Act does NOT apply, but PRA Section 87100 still applies
3. **Is this a contract?** -> Both Levine Act AND Section 1090 may apply
4. **Is this a commission appointment?** -> Neither Levine Act nor Section 1090 applies (employment exempt)
5. **Does the official have real property within 500-1000 feet of the subject property?** -> PRA Section 87100 applies with presumption
6. **Did a party/participant contribute >$500 to the decision-maker within 12 months?** -> Levine Act disclosure + recusal required (unless cured)

### Richmond-Specific Notes

- Richmond has its own campaign finance ordinance (Chapter 2.42) — exact per-donor limits need verification from Municode
- Richmond's 2012 ordinance referenced a $250 threshold for proceeding-related contributions, but the state Levine Act threshold (now $500) would apply as the controlling law
- Contra Costa County has a $2,500/cycle limit for county-level offices
- Richmond offers $25,000 in public matching funds with a $40,000 cap
